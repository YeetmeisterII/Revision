Lines

Vector Equation of a Line in 3D Space

To express a general point on the line, we give a point on the line and a direction.

For position vectors \(\textbf{a}\) and \(\textbf{b}\)

\(\underline{\textbf{r}} = \textbf{a} + \lambda \vec{AB}\)

Converting to Cartesian Equation

Here is a demonstration on converting \(\underline{\textbf{r}}\) to a cartesian equation.

Example:

  • If \(\textbf{a} = i + 2j + 3k\) and \(\textbf{b} = 0i + j + 2k\)
  • Then the equation of the line is \(\underline{\textbf{r}} = \textbf{a} + \lambda \vec{\textbf{ab}}\)
  • Which gives us \(\textbf{r} = (i + 2j + 3k) + \lambda (-i - j - 2k)\)
  • We can put this in the form \(\underline{\textbf{r}} = xi + yj + zk\)
  • \(\Rightarrow x = 1 - \lambda,\space y = 2 - \lambda,\space z = 3 - 2\lambda\)
  • \(\Rightarrow \lambda = 1 - x, \lambda = 2 - y, \lambda = \frac{3 - z}{2}\)
  • \(\Rightarrow 1 - x = 2 - y = \frac{3 - z}{2}\)

Intersecting Lines in 3D Space

Two lines in 3D space have 3 possible states.

  1. They"re parallel and never touch or touch at infinitely many point
  2. They intersect at a point
  3. They are in skew which means they meet at no points

Example:

  • \(\underline{\textbf{r}}_1 = (i + 3j + 0k) + \lambda (0i + j - k)\)
  • \(\underline{\textbf{r}}_2 = (2i + j + 3k) + \mu (i + 0j + k)\)
  • For these two lines to cross this statement must be true
  • \(x_1 = x_2 \cap y_1 = y_2 \cap z_1 = z_2\)
  • This leads to
  • \(1 = 2 + \mu \Rightarrow \mu = -1\)
  • \(3 + \lambda = 1 \Rightarrow \lambda = -2\)
  • \(-\lambda = 3 + \mu \Rightarrow -(-2) = 3 + (-1) \Rightarrow 2 = 2\)
  • Therefore the lines cross because the solutions are consistent
